Summary: Where We Landed

Green Energy remains a relatively small percentage of both the United States and the world energy portfolio. The reason of course, is that renewable power is expensive. But green energy generation has increased steadily over the last 3 decades.

For this project we chose to analyze data of energy produced by different sources and categorized them into green and conventional energy. Data shows energy produced from 1990-2018 by all 50 states.

As per our analysis in 1990, green energy production in the U.S stood at 1.7 billion (Mwh) and energy produced by conventional sources was 4.3 billion (Mwh). Green energy accounted for 29% of energy produced in 1990. Fast forward to 2018, this has risen to 34% of the energy produced in the U.S. Among the green energy sources wind and hydroelectric account for nearly 40% of energy produced in 2018. Energy production from wind farms has steadily increased since 1990, accounting for less than 1% of green energy to 20% in 2018.

In 2015 there were record investments in green energy and record capacity was added. Despite the massive investment, the global share of fossil fuels isnot shrinking very fast as expected. Green energy sources such as geothermal still account for a tiny share of the energy production, and there are certain factors that inhibit the growth of these projects. One factor could be the cost per watt for bigger projects.

Overall the green energy production has increased steadily over the last few decades, the green energy is growing from a very low base compared to conventional sources.
